Potato - Late Blight

Details

Late blight, caused by Phytophthora infestans, thrives in cool, wet conditions and spreads rapidly through infected plants and tubers. It is a globally significant disease and a major threat to potato and tomato crops.

Symptoms

Water-soaked, dark green lesions on leaves, progressing to brown or black patches.
White, fuzzy fungal growth on the underside of leaves during humid conditions.
Brown, dry rot lesions on potato tubers, which later become soft and odorous.

Treatment

Use resistant varieties of potatoes and follow crop rotation with non-host crops.
Destroy infected plant debris and volunteer plants.
Apply fungicides like chlorothalonil, mancozeb, or copper-based fungicides before or at the early signs of infection.

Squash - Powdery Mildew

Details

Caused by Podosphaera xanthii or Erysiphe cichoracearum, powdery mildew affects squash and other cucurbits. The disease thrives in warm, dry weather with high humidity.

Symptoms

White, powdery spots on leaves and stems that expand to cover large areas.
Premature yellowing and drying of leaves, reducing photosynthetic ability.
Stunted growth and reduced fruit yield.

Treatment

Grow resistant cultivars and ensure proper air circulation by spacing plants appropriately.
Apply sulfur-based fungicides or systemic fungicides like myclobutanil.
Use biocontrol agents like Ampelomyces quisqualis.

Strawberry - Leaf Scorch

Details

Leaf scorch, caused by Diplocarpon earlianum, primarily damages leaves, causing reduced vigor and fruit production. It is prevalent in humid, warm environments.

Symptoms

Purple to reddish-brown spots on leaves that coalesce, forming necrotic areas.
Scorched appearance of leaves with darkened veins.
Early leaf drop, leading to reduced plant productivity.

Treatment

Use resistant varieties and avoid overhead irrigation.
Apply fungicides like captan or thiophanate-methyl at the early stages of infection.
Remove infected plant debris to minimize overwintering pathogens.

Tomato - Bacterial Spot

Details

Caused by Xanthomonas campestris pv. vesicatoria, bacterial spot affects tomatoes and peppers. It spreads through seeds, water splashes, and contaminated equipment.

Symptoms

Small, water-soaked spots on leaves, stems, and fruits.
Necrotic lesions that merge and cause defoliation.
Fruit blemishes that lower market value.

Treatment

Use certified pathogen-free seeds and rotate crops with non-host plants.
Apply copper-based bactericides in combination with mancozeb for effective control.
Sanitize equipment and tools regularly.

Apple - Apple Scab

Details

Apple scab, caused by the fungus Venturia inaequalis, is one of the most economically important diseases of apples, reducing both fruit yield and quality.

Symptoms

Olive-green to brown velvety spots on leaves, which turn yellow and drop prematurely.
Scabby, rough lesions on fruits, causing cracking and deformities.
Dark lesions may appear on young twigs in severe infections.

Treatment

Plant scab-resistant apple cultivars whenever possible.
Apply fungicides, such as mancozeb or captan, during critical growth stages (e.g., pre-bloom to early fruit development).
Prune trees to improve air circulation and remove fallen leaves to reduce fungal inoculum.

Apple - Cedar Apple Rust

Details

Cedar apple rust, caused by the fungus Gymnosporangium juniperi-virginianae, requires both apple and cedar (or juniper) trees to complete its life cycle. It is common in areas where these species coexist.

Symptoms

Yellow-orange spots on apple leaves that later develop black dots (fungal structures).
Premature defoliation and reduced fruit quality.
Reddish-brown galls on cedar trees during wet weather.

Treatment

Avoid planting apples near cedar or juniper trees.
Apply fungicides like myclobutanil or mancozeb during the apple tree’s bloom period.
Remove galls from cedar trees before spores are released in the spring.

Grape - Esca (Black Measles)

Details

Esca, also known as Black Measles, is a complex disease caused by a group of fungal pathogens (Phaeomoniella chlamydospora and others). It primarily affects grapevines and causes vine decline.

Symptoms

Interveinal chlorosis on leaves, creating a tiger-stripe pattern.
Sunken, black lesions on berries that may ooze sap.
Stunted growth, dieback of shoots, and eventual vine death.

Treatment

Prune infected vines and remove diseased wood to reduce fungal spread.
Apply fungicides like fludioxonil as a preventive measure.
Avoid injuries during pruning and training to limit fungal entry points.

Tomato - Tomato Mosaic Virus

Details

The Tomato Mosaic Virus (ToMV) is a highly contagious virus that infects tomato plants and other solanaceous crops. It spreads through contaminated tools, hands, and soil.

Symptoms

Mottled, yellow-green patches on leaves.
Distorted, curling, or stunted leaves and reduced fruit set.
Fruit may have discoloration and uneven ripening.

Treatment

Plant resistant tomato varieties and use certified disease-free seeds.
Sterilize tools and avoid handling plants when they are wet.
Remove and destroy infected plants immediately to prevent the spread.

Symptoms

Yellowing and upward curling of leaves.

Treatment

Use whitefly-resistant tomato varieties and implement effective pest control measures.
Apply insecticides like imidacloprid to control whitefly populations.
Install physical barriers, such as insect-proof nets, to reduce whitefly entry.

Tomato - Spider Mites (Two-Spotted Spider Mite)

Details

Spider mites (Tetranychus urticae) are tiny arachnids that infest tomato plants, especially during hot, dry conditions. They feed on plant sap, causing damage to leaves and reducing yields.

Symptoms

Stippling or speckling on leaves, which later turn yellow or bronze.
Fine webbing on the underside of leaves.
Stunted plant growth and reduced fruit production.

Treatment

Use miticides such as abamectin or spiromesifen.
Introduce natural predators like lady beetles or predatory mites (Phytoseiulus persimilis).
Regularly spray plants with water to dislodge mites and reduce populations.

Tomato - Late Blight

Details

Late blight in tomatoes is caused by the oomycete Phytophthora infestans. It is a highly destructive disease that spreads rapidly under cool, wet conditions and can devastate crops if untreated.

Symptoms

Large, water-soaked lesions on leaves that turn brown and necrotic, often with a pale green border.
White, powdery fungal growth on the undersides of infected leaves during humid weather.
Dark, firm lesions on stems and fruits, leading to fruit rot and plant collapse.

Remove and destroy infected plant material immediately.
Avoid overhead irrigation and monitor weather conditions to apply fungicides preventively.

Corn (Maize) - Common Rust

Details

Common rust in corn is caused by the fungal pathogen Puccinia sorghi. It thrives in moderate temperatures (16–25°C or 60–77°F) and high humidity, spreading through windborne spores over long distances.

Symptoms

Small, round to oval pustules on leaves, initially yellowish-green, later turning reddish-brown.
Pustules may erupt and release rust-colored spores.
Severe infections can cause leaf yellowing, drying, and reduced photosynthesis, leading to yield losses.

Treatment

Use resistant maize hybrids to minimize susceptibility.
Apply fungicides like propiconazole, azoxystrobin, or pyraclostrobin if rust severity increases.
Practice crop rotation and remove crop debris to reduce overwintering spores.
Monitor fields regularly during conditions favorable for rust development.
